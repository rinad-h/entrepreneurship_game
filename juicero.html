Autopsy Report

Juicero

Time of Death: September 2017

Artifact

Background Check

Juicero was founded in 2013 and launched its high-tech juicer in 2016. The sleek countertop appliance was Wi-Fi connected and designed to work exclusively with Juicero’s proprietary, pre-packaged produce packs. With the press of a button, the machine would apply thousands of pounds of pressure to squeeze fresh juice directly into the glass, eliminating the need for cleanup or manual preparation. The company positioned this as a premium, modern solution for health-conscious consumers, marketing it around convenience, cleanliness, and superior juice quality.

What Happened

Juicero faced backlash for its high price and the revelation that the juice packs could be squeezed by hand, making the expensive machine largely unnecessary. Despite raising over $100 million in venture capital and receiving significant media attention, consumer adoption remained low and enthusiasm quickly faded. The company struggled to gain traction in the broader market, and by September 2017, Juicero shut down operations entirely.

Root Causes

  • Excessive price point: The Juicero machine sold for $699 (later lowered to $499), while individual juice packs cost around $5 each — far higher than what most consumers were willing to pay for at-home juice.
  • Over-engineered technology: Despite its sophistication, the machine’s main function was simply to squeeze pre-packaged juice bags, something consumers quickly discovered could be done by hand without the $699 device.
  • Restricted usability: Each pack contained a QR code that required the machine to connect to the internet for validation, and the device couldn’t be used with fresh fruits or vegetables, limiting flexibility and perceived value.
  • Niche target market: Juicero catered mainly to affluent, health-conscious consumers, a segment too narrow to sustain long-term growth.
  • Reliance on hype: Marketing emphasized high-tech design and Silicon Valley buzz rather than delivering clear consumer benefits tied to real pains, gains, or jobs to be done.

Mitigations

  • Ensure strong product-market fit by identifying real consumer pains, gains, or jobs to be done before building a high-tech solution.
  • Avoid over-engineering consumers won’t pay for technology that doesn’t provide meaningful benefits or solve a genuine problem.
  • Frame the value proposition around real, tangible benefits rather than creating an artificial sense of need, and validate it through customer discovery and early adopter feedback.
  • Clearly articulate what makes the product different and better than existing alternatives, emphasizing unique advantages that are meaningful to consumers.

Autopsy Findings

  • Premium pricing must be justified by clear, unique value that customers can easily perceive and that cannot be replicated by simpler or cheaper alternatives.
  • Products should address real consumer pains or create meaningful gains, rather than relying solely on flashy design, hype, or advanced technology.
  • Niche markets require careful validation through customer discovery and testing before attempting to scale, to ensure there is sufficient demand to sustain growth.
